BySage

Created a digital storefront that embraces an emerging fashion brand's humble beginnings to instill trust in an ever-growing customer base.

Overview

How can we redesign BySage's website to authentically represent its values and aesthetic?

BySage is a San Diego-based clothing brand built around limited drops and a loyal local following. When we started, the entire brand presence lived solely on Instagram, and the website only opened during launches—so first-time visitors had no way to browse the brand, read its story, or build any connection outside of drop windows.

Our team of four was brought on for a full website redesign. As lead, I owned:

  1. The reconstruction of site navigation, visual direction, and design across the product, collections, and home pages—focused on product transparency and brand storytelling.
  2. Leading our team through user interviews, design sprints, and usability testing.
  3. Proactively ideating with the PM and brand stakeholder—setting sprint goals for the product roadmap and implementing a plan for a viable MVP.

User Research

Listening before designing

We interviewed 23 individuals—from frequent repeat loyalists to potential customers who'd never bought from BySage. We needed that range of perspectives, and it surfaced three main issues:

Trust in Small Businesses

"How do I know this brand is legit? With a small label I can't always tell if the quality is there before I buy."

Unclear Product Information

"When a site buries the details, I give up. I don't have time to dig around for fit, materials, or policies between meetings."

Choice Overload on Mobile

"There's so much to scroll through and no guidance. I just want to find the standout piece without endless swiping."

Synthesizing the interviews, I grouped users into three personas defined by shopping frequency rather than demographics:

Frequent

Luna

20 · Fashion student · values sustainability & individuality

Wants versatile, year-round pieces that fit her budget and values. Frustrated by the lack of pieces tailored to a changing climate.

Non-frequent

Trevor

19 · Student athlete · values comfort & style

Discovered BySage once and hasn't returned. Needs durable, versatile clothing and a reason to re-engage outside of a drop.

Potential

Alex

20 · Student · values individuality

Found BySage on social media but has never purchased. Needs the brand's story and a trustworthy, intuitive shopping experience before buying.

That gap between strong brand loyalty and a weak web presence was the core problem—people loved BySage but had nowhere to engage with it between launches.

"Honestly the website is pretty plain compared to the Instagram. You also can't see when the page is locked for new drops, which makes me forget about it sometimes."

— Interview participant, SDSU undergrad

Information Architecture

Site architecture before pixels

Proper site architecture can turn endless scrolling into actionable clicks that lead to increase in sales. Our new structure gave every page a clear place so visitors could browse the brand's identity outside of active drops.

Final Design

A mobile-first shopping experience

The product page was rebuilt so customers can see everything that matters before buying—materials, fit, sizing, and reviews—directly addressing the trust gap that surfaced in research.

Mobile product page with reviews—built to earn first-time-buyer trust where most customers shop.

BySage's audience skews young—high school and college students who browse and buy from their phones. Responsive design wasn't optional. We designed mobile-first, then scaled up to desktop, making sure the shopping experience held together across both.

The Collections and About pages turned the brand's history into something visitors could explore year-round. The About page lays out BySage's story as a timeline—from its first ten hoodies in Chula Vista to its present-day drops.

Testing & Iteration

Task-based testing sharpened the build

We ran task-based usability testing with 10 participants, asking them to complete the following while thinking aloud:

  1. Shop for a hoodie
  2. Add it to the cart
  3. Find contact info
  4. Track an order

Two structural issues surfaced, and we resolved both:

Inconsistent Type Sizing

Users found text either too large or too small, with weak signifiers for interactive elements. We rebuilt the type scale across mobile and desktop and added clearer call-to-action indicators on every page.

Overwhelming Content Density

Media and text felt unbalanced and scaled too large, crowding the page. We rescaled content, split long copy into shorter paragraphs, and revisited the Collections and About pages with the client to keep only the most essential messaging.

Results

Where did we end up?

Giving BySage a persistent home drove engagement even outside of launch windows. After the redesign direction launched, the brand saw a 200% increase in Instagram followers, sold out its Spring 2024 collection within three minutes, and saw a 20%+ lift in traffic during drops.

Beyond the metrics, the founder could finally point people to a real website that represented his brand—not just an Instagram page that went dark between drops.

"Donovan immediately grasped and embraced our brand's vision, understanding the unique challenges of establishing trust and credibility for an emerging fashion brand. His redesign of the website not only improved navigation and usability but also significantly enhanced customer trust and engagement. His contributions have left a lasting impact on our brand's growth and reputation."

— Cameron Pitel, Founder of BySage

Reflection

What I took away

Designing within real constraints

One of the most valuable skills I built was developing a product under a real business's constraints. As designers we often shoot for the stars, but this project grounded me—the goal was a site that was accessible, feasible, and genuinely buildable for our client, not just impressive in a portfolio.

If I had more time, I'd push harder on the shopping flow itself. We focused our energy on the homepage, navigation, and brand identity; the product detail, cart, and checkout could have used another round of testing. That's where I'd go next.

Input Matters

Built a multi-modal interface for an assistive telepresence robot and conducted a 63-participant study to determine which control mode is the most intuitive and accessible.

    Role
    Human Factors Designer & Researcher
    Team
    2 Designers5 Engineers
    Skills
    User Research · UI Design · Interaction Logic · Experimental Design
    Tools
    Figma

    Overview

    How do you give someone their independence back?

    Chronic illnesses create barriers that prevent people from completing everyday tasks. The Contextual Robotics Institute is home to many labs that build telepresence robots providing people both assistance and agency—ensuring humans still retain a Sense of Control (SoC) when operating. With my colleagues at the UCSD Healthcare Robotics Lab, we designed a system for the Stretch RE2 Robot so that novice users can still complete their daily tasks.

    Background

    Stretch is a teleoperated assistive robot with unique physiology. It has a base, an arm positioned perpendicular to its base with a wrist and gripper, and two wide field-of-view cameras: an arm camera that gives users a clear line of sight when manipulating objects in space, and a base camera positioned at the top of the robot used during navigation mode. We installed a tablet near the robot's head to display the operator's face—promoting social interaction and enabling audio-visual communication between the operator and others in Stretch's environment.

    Design Audit

    Conducting A Design Audit

    Before prototyping, we audited the previous ideations our lab has developed and shipped since 2023. Across all versions, two issues were identified:

    Issue 01—Unstandardized Design System

    Inconsistent visual style guides and components that did not intuitively translate interaction logic or Stretch's physical attributes.

    Issue 02—Opaque Controls & Layout

    Components overlaid onto the camera feed weren't transparent and were arranged randomly, blocking the user's line of sight.

    Ideating

    Divide and Conquer

    The existing Stretch system supported two modes of operation: navigating Stretch through space and manipulating objects with its arm. We continued that direction—updating Navigation and Manipulation as separate tabs that users can toggle between.

    Issue 01—Standardizing the Design System

    Previous iterations were plagued with inconsistencies: different icons, varying text sizes, and vague controls. We threw out that playbook and introduced a standardized Visual Style Guide and Component Library in compliance with Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)—intuitive in both form and function.

    Issue 02—Increasing Visibility

    We made the overlaid controls transparent so Stretch's camera feed—and the user's line of sight—stayed clear.

    Cross-Platform

    What if Mouse & Key are not the right tools for the job?

    While debriefing our initial findings and literature review, we posed a new question: are there other control modalities that offer more intuitive operation and a stronger Sense of Control?

    Fortunately, video games had already solved this. Many popular game developers ship cross-platform compatibility—connecting players regardless of whether they're on console, PC, or mobile. Our initial scope broadened from designing a single interface to researching how different interaction modalities create a better user experience.

    Cross Platform Design

    How can we design a system that provides consistency across all three modalities: Mouse, Gamepad, and Haptic Omni Stylus?

    01—Modality-Specific Icons for Visual Feedback

    Standardizing the design system helped users visualize Stretch's physical attributes, but each modality had different interaction logic. Modality-specific icons served as indicators alongside function labels—Mouse users drag sliders, Gamepad users see controller button icons, and Omni users see hand gestures within a 3D space.

    02—Testing Light vs. Dark Mode

    We pushed both styles, tested them with colleagues, and landed on Dark Mode—it provided clearer legibility and reduced mental fatigue, our biggest concern for novice users operating Stretch for the first time.

    03—Redesigning Components for 3D Motion

    Our original arrow component didn't convey Stretch's arm moving through three axes. A new slider component unique to the arm's motion gave users a clearer sense of how their inputs translated to Stretch's physical movements.

    Final Designs

    One System, Two Modes, Three Control Modalities

    For each modality, our system provides visual feedback via Stretch's camera feeds and transparent control components that emphasize text legibility [1]. The modality icon [2] changes as users switch between controls. A miniature 3D model [3] shows Stretch's current orientation across three axes. The thumbnail [4] in the top left displays the arm camera's POV in Navigation mode and switches to the base camera's POV in Manipulation mode [5]. The top-right module [6] lets users pan the base camera in either mode.

    1. In Navigation Mode, move Stretch through terrain while monitoring the surrounding environment for obstructions.
    2. In Manipulation Mode, control Stretch's arm while using the base camera thumbnail to ensure the gripper path is clear.

    Preventing Slips and Errors

    Even experienced users can slip during task completion. We designed a control reference guide for each modality that users can toggle open at any time while operating Stretch.

    Study

    Conducting a 63-participant study in a cafe

    We ran a mixed-methods study to determine which control modality our 63 participants preferred across 130+ testing hours. The study spanned two locations: an Operation Room where participants remotely controlled Stretch, and a Cafe where Stretch operated alongside a researcher posing as a customer and another acting as cafe staff.

    Each participant was randomly assigned a modality, completed a training module, then ran a combination of navigation and manipulation tasks—handing a customer their menu, retrieving their order, and placing it on the coffee table.

    After completing the tasks, participants filled out a demographics form and a questionnaire measuring four dimensions:

    1. NASA Task Load Index (TLX)—How demanding the task felt
    2. System Usability Scale (SUS)—How easy the interface was to operate overall
    3. Perceived Safety (PS) scale—How participants felt when operating Stretch
    4. Sense of Control (SoC) scale—How much control participants felt they had over the robot

    Participants also answered open-ended questions about their experience with the system and their opinions on automation.

    Results

    Mouse outperformed Gamepad and Omni

    We analyzed all recorded video and audio data and used multiple linear regression (MLR) to assess the impact of control devices on task performance and SoC. We found SoC scores significantly correlated with completion rate and total task time.

    Reflection

    Wearing many hats

    01

    Scope creep is a feature, not a bug

    My role expanded well beyond UI design—and that was the right call. As the study evolved, so did my responsibilities. Every time the work needed something, I followed it there.

    02

    Designing meant doing

    I owned the interaction logic for all three modalities, scripted and produced tutorial videos so participants could onboard consistently, and contributed to experimental design decisions I hadn't anticipated at the start.

    03

    Close the Figma file when the problem asks you to

    The designers and researchers who add the most value are the ones willing to follow the problem wherever it goes—even when that means stepping away from Unity.
